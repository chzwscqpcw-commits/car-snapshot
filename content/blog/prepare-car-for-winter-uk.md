Winter in the UK does not announce itself politely. One week it is mild and damp; the next you are scraping ice off the windscreen in the dark, wondering whether the engine is going to start. A little preparation in autumn makes the difference between a car that handles winter comfortably and one that leaves you stranded.

This guide covers the practical steps every UK driver should take before the cold weather sets in.

## Check your battery

Cold weather is the number one killer of car batteries. A battery that starts the engine comfortably in September can fail completely by December. Low temperatures reduce a battery's output at the exact moment your car needs more power — cold engines are harder to turn over, and you are running headlights, heaters, and demisters constantly.

**What to do:**
- If your battery is over three years old, get it tested. Many garages and motor factors (Halfords, Euro Car Parts) offer free battery health checks.
- Clean any corrosion from the terminals using a wire brush. Corroded connections reduce the current reaching the starter motor.
- If the engine cranks slowly on the first start of the day, do not ignore it. A new battery costs GBP 60 to GBP 150 for most cars — far less than an emergency callout when it dies in a car park.

## Tyres: the 3mm rule

The legal minimum tread depth in the UK is 1.6mm across the central three-quarters of the tyre, but 1.6mm in winter is a gamble you do not want to take.

At 1.6mm, a tyre's ability to disperse water and grip cold tarmac is severely compromised. The difference between 1.6mm and 3mm in wet braking distances can be several car lengths. Most motoring organisations recommend treating 3mm as your practical winter minimum.

**What to do:**
- Check the tread on all four tyres using a depth gauge (under GBP 3) or the 20p coin test.
- Look for uneven wear, which can indicate alignment or suspension issues that become more dangerous on slippery roads.
- Check pressures when cold and adjust for the temperatures — tyre pressure drops by roughly 1 PSI for every 10-degree Celsius drop in temperature.
- Consider winter or all-season tyres if you live in a rural, hilly, or northern area. They use a softer compound that maintains grip below 7 degrees Celsius.

## Antifreeze and coolant

Engine coolant is a mix of water and antifreeze, and the ratio matters. Too little antifreeze and the coolant can freeze inside the engine block, causing catastrophic damage. Too much and it becomes less effective at transferring heat.

**What to do:**
- Check the coolant level in the expansion tank (engine cold). Top up if it is below the minimum mark.
- Use an antifreeze tester (a few pounds from any motor factor) to check the freeze protection level. It should protect to at least minus 15 degrees Celsius for most of the UK, or minus 25 if you are in Scotland or exposed highland areas.
- If the coolant looks brown, murky, or has debris floating in it, it may need replacing. Coolant should be changed every few years as it degrades over time.

## Screenwash concentration

Summer-diluted screenwash will freeze in winter, leaving you with no way to clear a filthy windscreen — a genuine safety hazard on motorways. Road salt and spray from other vehicles coats windscreens constantly in winter.

**What to do:**
- Drain any remaining summer-strength screenwash or top up with a concentrated winter mix.
- Use a quality screenwash rated to at least minus 10 degrees Celsius. Cheap supermarket screenwash sometimes freezes before reaching its claimed rating.
- Check that the washer nozzles are clear and properly aimed. A blocked nozzle is easily fixed with a pin.

## Lights: your most important winter tool

In winter, you drive in the dark far more than in summer. Many commuters leave and return home in darkness. Working lights are not optional — they are critical for both seeing and being seen.

**What to do:**
- Check every light on the car: headlights (dipped and main beam), indicators, brake lights, rear fog lights, reversing lights, number plate lights, and front fog lights if fitted.
- Clean the light lenses. Road grime and salt spray can reduce light output significantly without you noticing the gradual decline.
- Check your headlight alignment. Misaligned headlights dazzle oncoming drivers and reduce your own visibility. If your beams seem to point in odd directions, most garages can adjust them for a few pounds.

If your MOT is due soon, sorting lights now avoids the most common MOT failure category. Check your [MOT due date](/mot-check) and review any [advisories from your last test](/) to see if lighting was flagged.

## Wiper blades and the demister

Visibility in winter is already compromised by short days, rain, fog, and spray. Worn wipers and a struggling demister make it worse.

**What to do:**
- Replace wiper blades if they are streaking, smearing, or juddering. Winter is the hardest season on wipers, and a fresh set costs under GBP 15 for most cars.
- Check that your heater and demister work effectively. A car that takes 20 minutes to clear a fogged windscreen on a cold morning has a problem — possibly a cabin filter that needs replacing or a heater matrix issue.
- Carry a microfibre cloth to clear interior condensation quickly.

## Build a winter emergency kit

UK winters produce ice, snow, flooding, and fog — sometimes all in the same week. A small kit in the boot gives you options when things go wrong.

**Essential items:**
- Ice scraper and de-icer spray
- Torch with fresh batteries (or a wind-up torch)
- Fully charged power bank for your phone
- Warm blanket or sleeping bag
- Warm hat and gloves (separate from ones you wear while driving)
- Bottled water and a non-perishable snack
- Hi-vis vest
- Jump leads

**If you regularly drive in areas prone to snow:**
- A small folding shovel
- A bag of cat litter or sand (for traction if stuck)
- A tow rope

## Check your MOT and tax status

Winter is a bad time to discover your MOT has expired. Driving without a valid MOT invalidates your insurance, and no one wants to deal with that on top of an icy morning breakdown.

Run a [free vehicle check](/) to confirm your MOT expiry date and [tax status](/tax-check). If your MOT is due in the coming months, book early — garages get busy in the run-up to Christmas. Review the [most common MOT failures](/blog/most-common-mot-failures) and address any obvious issues before your appointment.

## Consider your car's emissions and running costs

Winter driving increases fuel consumption — cold starts, heater use, and congested roads all take their toll. If you are concerned about running costs, check your car's [estimated running costs and valuation](/car-valuation) to understand where your money goes.

If you drive in London, confirm your [ULEZ compliance](/ulez-check) status. The charge applies year-round, and forgetting about it during a winter trip into the zone is an expensive mistake.

## Start now, not in December

The best time to prepare your car for winter is before winter arrives. An October afternoon spent checking tyres, topping up fluids, and testing lights is a small investment against the cold, dark months ahead. By the time frost is on the ground, you want to be ready — not scrambling for a garage appointment.
