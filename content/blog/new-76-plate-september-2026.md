On 1 September 2026, the new 76 plate arrives on UK roads. If you are buying a new car, thinking about selling your current one, or simply curious about what those numbers mean, here is everything you need to know about the September plate change and why it matters more than you might think.

## What the 76 actually means

UK registration plates follow a consistent system introduced in 2001. The two-digit number in the middle of the plate — the age identifier — tells you exactly when the vehicle was first registered.

The system works on a simple six-monthly cycle:

- **March plates** use the last two digits of the year directly. So a 26 plate means March to August 2026.
- **September plates** add 50 to the year. So 76 means September 2026 to February 2027 (50 + 26 = 76).

The 76 plate will appear on all new vehicles registered from 1 September 2026 through to 28 February 2027. After that, the 27 plate takes over in March 2027.

For a full explanation of how the entire plate system works — including the meaning of the letters and the history of older formats — read our complete guide to [what your UK number plate means](/blog/what-uk-number-plate-means).

## How the full plate breaks down

A typical 76 plate will look something like **AB76 CDE**. Each part carries information:

- **AB** — The memory tag, indicating the DVLA local office area where the car was first registered. The first letter represents the region (L for London, B for Birmingham, S for Scotland, and so on).
- **76** — The age identifier, confirming the car was registered in the September 2026 to February 2027 window.
- **CDE** — Three random letters assigned to make the plate unique. The DVLA withholds combinations that could spell offensive words.

This system generates more than enough unique combinations to cover UK demand well into the 2050s.

## Why plate changes drive used car bargains

The plate change is not just about new cars. It creates a ripple effect through the entire used car market that savvy buyers can exploit.

### The depreciation trigger

When the 76 plate launches on 1 September, every car on the 26 plate (March to August 2026) becomes one registration cycle older. In practical terms, the car is no different on 31 August and 1 September, but the market perception shifts. A 26-plate car is now "last plate" rather than "current plate."

This matters because a significant chunk of depreciation is tied to the perceived age of a vehicle. Dealers price their stock based partly on the plate, and buyers naturally gravitate towards the latest registration. The result is that 26-plate cars — and the plates before them — often become available at lower prices in September and October.

### Motivated sellers

Many drivers trade in their cars around plate changes, upgrading to the latest model. This floods the market with part-exchange stock, giving dealers more inventory to shift and increasing competition. Private sellers doing the same may accept lower offers to fund their new purchase.

If you are looking to buy a used car, the weeks either side of a plate change are often the [best time to buy](/blog/best-time-to-buy-used-car-uk). The combination of increased supply and downward price pressure on outgoing plates works in the buyer's favour.

### Nearly-new opportunities

Some of the best value appears in the "nearly new" segment. Demonstrator vehicles, pre-registered stock, and cars returned from short-term leases often appear on dealer forecourts around the plate change as dealerships refresh their inventory. These cars may have covered only a few thousand miles but carry significant discounts over brand-new equivalents.

## What to check on a newly registered car

Whether you are buying brand new on the 76 plate or picking up a used car in the plate-change window, there are important checks to make.

### For brand-new cars

A new car is not immune from problems. Before driving away from the dealership:

- **Check the paperwork.** Confirm the V5C application has been submitted, your details are correct, and the registration matches what is on the car.
- **Inspect the car thoroughly.** Look for paint defects, stone chips from transport, interior marks, and that all electronics work properly. Any issues should be documented and agreed for rectification before you leave.
- **Confirm the warranty.** Understand exactly what is covered, for how long, and whether servicing must be done at franchise dealers to maintain it.
- **Check for safety recalls.** Even new models can be subject to manufacturer recalls. Run a [free vehicle check](/) to see if any recalls apply to your specific make and model.

### For used cars around the plate change

The plate-change period brings more cars to market, but it also attracts sellers trying to shift problematic vehicles among the rush. Protect yourself:

- **Run a full vehicle check.** Enter the registration on [Free Plate Check](/) to see the MOT history, mileage records, tax status, and vehicle details. Our [car valuation tool](/car-valuation) can also help you gauge whether the asking price is fair.
- **Check the mileage progression.** MOT records show the mileage at each test. Any drops or suspicious jumps are red flags. Read our guide on [how to spot a clocked car](/blog/how-to-spot-a-clocked-car) to know what to look for.
- **Verify the V5C.** The registration document should show the seller as the registered keeper. If it does not, ask why. A missing V5C is a warning sign.
- **Check for outstanding finance.** A car with unpaid finance can be repossessed from you even if you bought it in good faith. This is especially important for nearly-new cars, which are frequently financed.

## Beyond the plate: the bigger picture

The twice-yearly plate change has been part of UK motoring since 2001, and the pattern will continue for decades. The DVLA designed the current system to run until at least 2051, by which point we will be on 01 plates again — with an entirely different meaning from the 2001 originals.
